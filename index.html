About Me.

Hi, I’m Rajlaxmi.

I’m an aspiring quantum physicist — currently based in Paris — who also happens to enjoy singing (off-key, most days), solving Rubik’s cubes a little too obsessively, and baking as a coping mechanism. With a background in physics and a soft spot for creativity, I’m trying to make sense of superconducting circuits and hybrid quantum systems — mostly through long days in cleanrooms and even longer nights debugging code and analysing quantum transport measurements. I’ve come to appreciate the beauty in color-coded data and the strangely calming process of cryogenic experiments.

I’ve learned (sometimes the hard way) how to avoid getting etched, both in the lab and in life. Whether it's modeling an amplifier, baking a cookie the size of a wafer, or singing to a cryostat just for fun — I’m just doing my best to keep learning while being curious and enjoying the ride, one messy experiment at a time.

Fractional Quantum Anomalous Hall Effect in Rhombohedral Graphene

CEA Saclay

"Rhombohedral graphene is rewriting the rules of quantum physics—it's not just a superconductor that lets electrons flow with zero resistance, but also a surprise party magnet. Four or five layers of graphene when stacked just right, they form a bizarre quantum mash-up: a chiral superconductor that also shows intrinsic magnetism. Superconductors typically hate magnetic fields, but this one seems to be a magnet, switching between superconducting states under a magnetic field." Coming Soon.

Effect of Dielectric Thickness on Rubrene Based Phototransistors

IISER Tirupati

"I built tiny light detectors using rubrene—a molecule that sounds like a Pokémon but acts like a photo-powered switch. I played with the thickness of the dielectric to see how it messes with performance. Turns out, in phototransistors, size really does matter. Lots of light, some electricity, and just the right amount of lab drama."

Towards Hybrid Qubits in Bilayer Graphene Quantum Dots

RWTH Aachen University

"I trapped a single electron in bilayer graphene, stuck it next to a superconductor, and watched what weird quantum things it would do—like sharing Cooper pairs, splitting them, or tunneling like a ninja. Basically, I built a tiny playground for electrons and let quantum physics go wild. Tools used: Whole lot of patience."
